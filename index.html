Beyond Educational Deprivation: Resident Bypass as a Source of Guideline-Discordant Clinical Decisions and Measurable Patient-Level Harm

Target Journal: The Joint Commission Journal on Quality and Patient Safety
Status: Complete First Draft — April 18, 2026
Note: This document is fully de-identified. All provider names, patient names, institution names, MRN numbers, accession numbers, and specific calendar dates have been removed. Role-based descriptors are used throughout.

STRUCTURED ABSTRACT

Background

Resident bypass — the practice whereby attending physicians redirect clinical communication, orders, or decision-making without resident involvement — has been characterized in the graduate medical education literature primarily as a barrier to competency attainment, disrupting deliberate practice, entrustable professional activity progression, and ACGME milestone development. This framing is incomplete. Existing literature has not characterized bypass as a patient safety phenomenon capable of producing guideline-discordant clinical decisions through a traceable causal mechanism, nor has it addressed the inverse supervision risk: that attending decisions made during bypass events may be less guideline-concordant than the resident assessments they displace. No existing patient safety reporting framework, quality review mechanism, or institutional accountability structure is designed to detect, evaluate, or remediate this risk.

Case Summary

An 85-year-old woman was admitted to an academic community hospital internal medicine service for emergent operative fixation of a traumatic right intertrochanteric hip fracture. At 3:07 AM on the day of admission, the admitting PGY-3 resident completed a comprehensive history and physical examination documenting all relevant cardiac risk considerations — a BNP of 202.8 pg/mL, a mild troponin elevation with downtrend, an abnormal electrocardiogram, and intermittent non-exertional nocturnal chest discomfort — and reached the explicit, verbatim, guideline-concordant conclusion that no evidence of STEMI, decompensated heart failure, or other unstable cardiac condition requiring routine delay of time-sensitive hip fracture surgery was present. A calculated RCRI score of 0 provided objective quantification of low perioperative cardiac risk. The admission order set simultaneously initiated echocardiography, conservative perioperative fluid management at a keep-vein-open rate with an explicit volume overload monitoring caveat, multimodal analgesia, renal-protective medication dosing, non-pharmacologic delirium precautions, and social work evaluation for bereavement support.

Prior to completion of the history and physical examination, the supervising attending had communicated the requirement for cardiology clearance before surgery, citing concern for undiagnosed heart failure. The resident pushed back directly; he was overruled. He ordered the cardiology consultation at 4:54 AM and documented his contrary clinical conclusion in the permanent medical record. At approximately 6:00 AM, the attending reviewed the history and physical examination — including its explicit contrary conclusion — and repeated the requirement without engaging a single element of the resident's reasoning. No documentation of her clinical rationale was entered in the medical record. The resident's overnight shift ended.

The echocardiogram ordered by the resident was finalized at 12:58 PM, demonstrating a hyperdynamic ejection fraction of 71% with diastolic function formally read as normal, categorically excluding the conditions the attending had cited as clinical concern. The daytime hospitalist's progress note documented "cardiology clearance pending" as a surgical gate and re-ordered maintenance fluids at 75 mL/hr — confirming the conservative fluid strategy across two providers and two shifts. The orthopedic consult note documented that the patient was "awaiting cardiac clearance." The cardiology consultation — required at approximately 6:00 AM and ordered at 4:54 AM — was not completed until the admitting resident returned to overnight coverage at 7:00 PM and contacted cardiology directly, despite the echocardiogram having been available in the chart for six hours.

The cardiology consultant evaluated the patient with the finalized echocardiogram available and documented: "She is stable from cardiovascular perspective to have general anesthesia and orthopedic surgery in the morning as planned. Perioperative cardiovascular risk is low, cannot be lowered further." His recommendation included: "Cleared for surgery tomorrow." This conclusion was identical in clinical substance to the admitting resident's 3:07 AM documentation. Twenty-eight minutes later, the supervising attending attested the history and physical examination, documenting agreement with the resident's findings and plan. Surgery did not occur that evening.

Both fluid orders placed during the pre-operative period — the resident's keep-vein-open order and the daytime hospitalist's maintenance order — reflected surgical anticipation orders calibrated to cover a pre-operative window each provider expected to be brief. Neither was designed as open-ended maintenance. The cardiology clearance requirement, by extending the operative delay beyond any provider's anticipated surgical window, systematically exhausted both orders. At 0015 on the following morning, the bedside nurse documented a blood pressure of 79/44 mmHg in a sleeping, asymptomatic patient and notified both residents via SecureChat at 12:42 AM.

The PGY-1 resident, responding to the SecureChat notification, independently ordered a 1-liter intravenous lactated Ringer's bolus — entered under his own name in the electronic health record — without knowledge of the admitting resident's graduated fluid protocol, the conservative fluid strategy confirmed across two prior shifts, or the attending's earlier expressed concern for possible heart failure. Both residents and the bedside nurse subsequently met at the bedside. The admitting resident assessed the patient directly — noting the absence of compensatory tachycardia, preserved oxygenation, prior systolic blood pressure range of 108–155 mmHg, and a clinical picture consistent with hypovolemia from fracture hematoma blood loss compounded by sedating medications and absent maintenance fluids — and communicated a revised graduated fluid protocol in the SecureChat: 500 mL bolus with structured reassessment, escalation to an additional 250 mL if systolic blood pressure remained below 110 mmHg, and resumption of maintenance at 75 mL/hr once the hemodynamic threshold was met.

Following this communication, the supervising attending appeared in person at the admitting resident's workstation, announced that she had ordered the 1-liter bolus, and walked away. No clinical dialogue occurred. No engagement with the graduated protocol took place. No explanation of clinical reasoning was offered. The admitting resident documented the attending's intervention in the SecureChat — the only record, under any authorship, of the attending's clinical decision in the permanent medical record. The attending's decision does not appear in the electronic health record under her own name. The hemodynamic threshold specified in the resident's graduated protocol — systolic blood pressure above 110 mmHg — was subsequently achieved, and the resident implemented maintenance fluids at 75 mL/hr.

The supervising attending attested the admitting resident's overnight progress note the following morning. The patient underwent operative fixation of the hip fracture later that day without complications.

Analysis

The 2014 ACC/AHA Guideline on Perioperative Cardiovascular Evaluation explicitly directs the clinical team to proceed with emergent surgery without pre-operative cardiac evaluation in the absence of active cardiac conditions. This patient met no defined criteria for an active cardiac condition at any point in her hospitalization. The cardiology consultant who confirmed this conclusion reviewed the complete echocardiographic and laboratory dataset — including the moderately elevated pulmonary artery systolic pressure of 50 mmHg — and found no barrier to surgery. The attending's stated concern for possible heart failure was the primary clinical rationale most strongly supporting the admitting resident's conservative fluid approach; aggressive volume loading in elderly patients with suspected elevated filling pressures is a recognized precipitant of acute decompensated heart failure. The attending's fluid management decision was internally contradictory to her own stated clinical concern. Both of the attending's clinical interventions — the cardiology clearance requirement and the 1-liter bolus confirmation — were independently discordant with the evidence base governing their respective clinical domains.

The nocturnal hemodynamic episode was plausibly, though not definitively, downstream of multiple contributing delays: primarily the attending's guideline-discordant cardiology clearance requirement, and secondarily the daytime team's failure to contact cardiology during daytime hours despite the consultation having been required and the echocardiogram having been available since 12:58 PM. The primary hemodynamic substrate — approximately 750–800 mL of occult hemorrhage into the fracture hematoma over the pre-operative period, documented by the admitting resident in the overnight note — reflects the well-established physiologic consequence of prolonged pre-operative delay in elderly hip fracture patients.

Five distinct systems failure modes are identified: removal of a documented safety barrier without institutional review; exploitation of the shift handoff as a structural vulnerability; fait accompli bypass as a patient safety mechanism foreclosing clinical dialogue at the moment of disclosure; an undocumented attending clinical decision attributable only through the resident's relay message; and the complete absence of institutional detection, reporting, or review mechanisms across the full causal chain.

Conclusions

Resident bypass is not a supervisory style or a communication preference. This case documents it as a patient safety failure mode with a reproducible mechanism, a traceable causal chain from bypass decision to patient-level harm, and an institutional detection gap that existing supervisory and reporting frameworks are structurally unable to address. Six recommendations are offered: incorporating bypass into patient safety event reporting frameworks; requiring attending documentation when overriding formally documented resident assessments; establishing guideline concordance review for attending orders that override resident assessments in time-sensitive surgical patients; aligning shift handoff policy with bypass prevention; integrating bypass identification into ACGME CLER self-study and faculty development curricula; and closing the feedback loop between consultant assessments and the resident clinical reasoning they evaluate. The institutional infrastructure to implement these recommendations does not yet exist. Building it is the work this case calls for.

SECTION 1 — INTRODUCTION

Clinical supervision in teaching hospitals is designed as a safety system. The attending physician's oversight of resident clinical decisions represents the terminal check in a layered defensive architecture — the mechanism by which trainee reasoning is verified, refined, and, when necessary, corrected before it reaches the patient. This architecture rests on a foundational assumption: that attending decisions represent an improvement on, or at minimum a verification of, the resident's assessment. When that assumption holds, supervision functions as intended. When it does not — when the attending's substituted judgment is less guideline-concordant, less internally consistent, or less evidence-aligned than the resident's — the supervisory architecture inverts. The terminal check does not improve the clinical decision. It displaces one that was better.

Resident bypass is the practice whereby attending physicians redirect clinical communication, orders, or decision-making to consultants, nurses, or patients without resident involvement. It has been recognized in the graduate medical education literature as a barrier to competency attainment, documented to disrupt deliberate practice, impair supervisor-trainee trust calibration, and transmit a hidden curriculum message that resident judgment is presumptively inadequate regardless of demonstrated competence. The entrustment framework articulated by Ten Cate — in which attending physicians make entrustment decisions based on iteratively observed, authentic clinical performance — requires exactly the kind of supervised independent practice that bypass systematically denies. The ACGME Common Program Requirements mandate graduated autonomy; bypass is structurally incompatible with that mandate.

These educational harms are real and well-documented. They are, however, incomplete as a characterization of what bypass produces. The existing literature on resident bypass has not characterized it as a patient safety phenomenon — one capable of generating not merely educational deprivation but proximate, documentable, patient-level adverse outcomes through a traceable causal mechanism. This gap in the literature is significant. Framing bypass exclusively as an educational failure produces exclusively educational remediation: faculty development programs, entrustment frameworks, milestone oversight. These interventions do not address the inverse supervision risk — attending clinical decisions made without resident engagement that are less safe than the decisions they replace — because that risk has not been named, characterized, or incorporated into existing patient safety reporting or quality review frameworks.

The patient safety literature has extensively characterized the risks of insufficient attending oversight of residents. Farnan and colleagues, in a systematic review of supervision and patient safety, documented the relationship between inadequate supervision and adverse outcomes in training environments. The ACGME's Clinical Learning Environment Review program — CLER — explicitly evaluates supervision culture as a patient safety variable, identifying it as one of six focus areas requiring institutional assessment in teaching hospitals. What neither the supervision literature nor the CLER framework has addressed is the structurally opposite risk: that attending physicians, in bypassing residents, can introduce clinical decisions that are guideline-discordant, internally contradictory to their own stated clinical concerns, and capable of producing patient-level harm through mechanisms that existing institutional reporting systems are not designed to detect.

We present a case from an academic community hospital internal medicine service that documents this inverse risk with a degree of evidentiary precision rarely available in case-based patient safety literature. The case involves an 85-year-old woman admitted for emergent operative fixation of a traumatic intertrochanteric hip fracture, in whom two discrete bypass events — separated by approximately 19 hours and employing the same behavioral mechanism — produced a traceable causal chain from bypass decision to patient-level harm. The admitting resident's clinical assessments were documented in the permanent medical record at the time they were made: a comprehensive history and physical examination completed at 3:07 AM explicitly applying ACC/AHA perioperative guideline reasoning and reaching a guideline-concordant conclusion, and a graduated fluid management protocol developed through direct bedside evaluation and communicated through a documented clinical communication channel. Both assessments were bypassed through in-person, unilateral determinations delivered without clinical dialogue. Both bypass events are documentable from the permanent medical record. The specialist consulted to address the attending's stated concern confirmed the resident's assessment. The attending attested the resident's history and physical examination — documenting her agreement with his findings and plan — 28 minutes after that confirmation.

This case does not present bypass as a communication style or a supervisory preference. It presents bypass as a systems failure with a documentable adverse outcome pathway, a reproducible behavioral mechanism, and an institutional detection gap that existing patient safety infrastructure is not equipped to address. The argument has implications beyond graduate medical education: for patient safety reporting frameworks, quality improvement infrastructure, institutional accountability mechanisms, and the ACGME's Clinical Learning Environment Review program, which is specifically charged with evaluating whether teaching hospitals have integrated patient safety culture with the realities of clinical supervision.

SECTION 2 — CASE PRESENTATION

An 85-year-old woman was brought to the emergency department of an academic community hospital following a mechanical fall. She reported mistepping and losing her balance, falling onto her right side without loss of consciousness. Imaging confirmed a mildly displaced right intertrochanteric femur fracture, a right distal radial metadiaphyseal fracture with mild dorsal rotation and impaction, and a left index finger laceration requiring sutured repair; the right wrist was splinted. Emergent operative fixation of the hip fracture was indicated. Relevant medical history included depression, insomnia, chronic mild thrombocytopenia, and chronic kidney disease stage 3a with stable renal function. The patient's husband of 64 years had died two days prior to her presentation.

Before 3:07 AM, Day 2 — Pre-H&P Verbal Interaction (Bypass Event 1, Part A)

Prior to completing the admission history and physical examination, and after evaluating the patient, the supervising attending communicated her clinical concern to the admitting PGY-3 resident in person: the BNP of 202.8 pg/mL and troponin elevation raised concern for undiagnosed heart failure, and she required a cardiology consultation for preoperative clearance before surgery could proceed. The admitting resident pushed back directly, arguing that preoperative cardiology clearance was not warranted for a patient with a time-sensitive hip fracture and no clinical evidence of an active cardiac condition. The attending responded by restating her concern for undiagnosed heart failure. The requirement stood. No documentation of this exchange exists in the medical record.

Day 2, 3:07 AM — Admission Assessment (PGY-3)

The admitting internal medicine resident, a PGY-3, completed a comprehensive history and physical examination and entered a full admission order set. The clinical impression enumerated the patient's diagnoses in full and explicitly identified four cardiac risk considerations bearing on perioperative management: a mildly elevated BNP of 202.8 pg/mL, a mild troponin elevation with documented downtrend (0.06 to 0.05 ng/mL), an abnormal electrocardiogram, and a history of intermittent non-exertional nocturnal burning chest discomfort. Each finding was explicitly contextualized within the clinical picture. The possibility of Takotsubo cardiomyopathy was noted and addressed. The resident calculated and documented an RCRI score of 0, corresponding to a 30-day major cardiac event risk of 0.5–3.9%, and a Preoperative Mortality Predictor score of 9 reflecting low perioperative mortality risk. The troponin elevation was characterized as most consistent with demand myocardial injury from the acute fracture and hospitalization rather than plaque rupture, given the absence of current chest pain, dyspnea, and pulmonary edema on chest radiograph. The BNP was used for risk stratification and perioperative planning rather than as evidence of decompensated heart failure.

The resident's documented clinical conclusion, preserved verbatim in the permanent medical record, read: "there is no current evidence in the chart of STEMI, decompensated HF, or another unstable cardiac condition requiring routine delay of time-sensitive hip fracture surgery." Documented clinical priorities were timely operative repair of the hip fracture, pain control, focused preoperative optimization, and delirium and fall risk mitigation.

The admission order set addressed each identified risk domain systematically. Cardiac workup orders included transthoracic echocardiography, a repeat electrocardiogram, and a cardiology consultation — all pending. Fluid management orders specified low-rate intravenous fluids at a keep-vein-open rate of 15 mL per hour while the patient remained NPO, with an explicit caveat to monitor for volume overload. Analgesic orders included scheduled acetaminophen 650 mg orally every six hours and morphine 2 mg intravenously every two hours as needed for severe pain while NPO and preoperative, with explicit monitoring parameters for sedation, hypotension, and respiratory depression, and a naloxone order for respiratory rate below 10. Renal-protective orders included renal dosing of all medications, avoidance of NSAIDs, and avoidance of unnecessary intravenous contrast. Electrolyte targets were specified: potassium above 4.0 mEq/L and magnesium above 2.0 mg/dL, with instructions to avoid adding QT-prolonging medications given the home medication list. Pharmacologic anticoagulation and baclofen were held preoperatively. Home medications including fluoxetine, quetiapine, and trazodone were continued. Non-pharmacologic delirium precautions were explicitly ordered, encompassing reorientation, hearing support, sleep protection, and early post-operative mobilization. Daily complete blood count, daily basic metabolic panel, urine output monitoring, and mental status evaluation each shift were ordered. Consultations for orthopedic surgery, palliative care, and social work — the latter specifically for bereavement support and discharge planning — were placed.

Day 2, 4:54 AM — Cardiology Consultation Ordered (PGY-3)

The admitting resident placed the cardiology consultation order. The timing of this order reflects clinical reasoning rather than procedural urgency: the consulting cardiologist would not evaluate the patient until daytime hours and not before the echocardiogram was finalized, making the precise time of order placement clinically inconsequential. The order represented the resident's compliance with a directive he had already contested on clinical grounds and had documented his contrary reasoning about in the permanent medical record less than two hours earlier.

Day 2, ~6:00 AM — Attending Review (Bypass Event 1, Part B)

At the end of the admitting resident's overnight shift, the supervising attending reviewed the chart. This was the second interaction between the attending and the admitting resident regarding this patient's cardiac management. The attending had already stated her requirement for cardiology clearance before the H&P was written; the resident had pushed back directly and been overruled. She had now read the resident's explicit, timestamped, guideline-concordant conclusion — including the verbatim statement that no evidence of STEMI, decompensated heart failure, or other unstable cardiac condition requiring routine delay was present — and the comprehensive order set that operationalized that conclusion. She repeated the requirement for cardiology clearance prior to surgery. No question was posed to the resident. No element of the resident's documented reasoning was addressed, challenged, or incorporated. The attending was physically present with the H&P's author at both interactions. At neither moment did clinical dialogue occur. The resident's overnight shift concluded.

Day 2, 10:46 AM — Daytime Hospitalist Progress Note

The daytime hospitalist provider — a nurse practitioner — documented a progress note and re-ordered maintenance intravenous lactated Ringer's solution at 75 mL per hour, consistent with the admitting resident's conservative fluid strategy. Under the plan, the daytime hospitalist explicitly documented "cardiology clearance pending" — formally establishing the cardiology clearance requirement as a surgical gate in the written medical record. The note also documented "orthopedics planning on surgical fixation," consistent with an anticipated surgical timeline that the fluid order was calibrated to cover.

Physical examination documented a clinically euvolemic patient: no jugular venous distension, no pedal edema, clear lung fields, normal heart sounds, no murmur.

The medication administration record documents the daytime hospitalist's 75 mL/hr fluid order beginning at approximately 10:57 AM and ending at approximately 4:45 PM — a duration of approximately six hours. Both the admitting resident's keep-vein-open order (15 mL/hr, approximately 6.5 hours) and the daytime hospitalist's maintenance order (75 mL/hr, approximately 6 hours) reflected surgical anticipation orders calibrated to cover a pre-operative window each provider reasonably expected to be brief. Neither order was designed as open-ended maintenance, because neither provider anticipated a pre-operative course extending beyond their coverage period. The clinical or administrative reason for the daytime hospitalist's fluid order end time has not been established from the available record.

Day 2, 12:58 PM — Echocardiogram Finalized

The transthoracic echocardiogram ordered by the admitting resident at 3:07 AM was signed and available in the electronic health record. The interpreting cardiologist reported a hyperdynamic ejection fraction of 71%, with diastolic function formally read as normal. Left atrial volume index was 28 mL/m², within the normal reference range, arguing against chronically elevated filling pressures. The average E/e' ratio was 12, below the threshold of 14 associated with elevated filling pressures. No structural pathology was identified. Wall motion was normal with no regional abnormalities. Mild mitral and tricuspid regurgitation were noted, consistent with age-related annular changes. Pulmonary artery systolic pressure was moderately increased at 50 mmHg by Doppler; estimated right atrial pressure was 8 mmHg; inferior vena cava was normal in size with blunted respirophasic variation. A trace pericardial effusion was identified. Mild concentric hypertrophy was present. Rhythm was confirmed as normal sinus rhythm.

The echocardiographic result resolved the attending's stated concern for undiagnosed heart failure: heart failure with reduced ejection fraction was categorically excluded by the hyperdynamic ejection fraction, and heart failure with preserved ejection fraction was unsupported by the formally normal diastolic function, normal left atrial volume index, and E/e' ratio within the normal reference range. No ACC/AHA-defined active cardiac condition was present by any echocardiographic criterion. The cardiology consultation required at approximately 6:00 AM had not yet been completed at the time of echo finalization, despite the result having been available in the chart for the preceding six hours.

Day 2, 3:14 PM — Orthopedic Consultation

The orthopedic service evaluated the patient and confirmed the operative plan for right hip ORIF. The consultation note documented under plan: "Patient is awaiting cardiac clearance." Surgery was not yet scheduled.

Day 2, 3:35 PM — Palliative Care Consultation

Palliative care evaluated the patient. A goals of care discussion was conducted with the patient and family over approximately 50 minutes. The patient consented to DNR/DNI status, to be withheld during surgery and restored after. Palliative Performance Scale was documented at 40%. An advance directive was created.

Day 2, ~4:45 PM — Fluid Order Expiration

The daytime hospitalist's maintenance intravenous fluid order expired. The patient had no intravenous fluids running from this point. She remained pre-operative, NPO, and immobilized with a traumatic hip fracture and distal radius fracture.

Day 2, 7:00 PM — Admitting Resident Returns to Overnight Coverage (PGY-3)

The admitting resident returned to overnight coverage. The echocardiogram had been available in the chart for approximately six hours. The cardiology consultation — required by the attending approximately thirteen hours earlier and ordered by the resident at 4:54 AM — had not been completed. When the admitting resident contacted the cardiology office, he was informed that no prior consult call had been placed. The cardiology nurse practitioner independently confirmed the absence of prior contact. The admitting resident contacted cardiology directly.

Day 2, 8:06 PM — Cardiology Evaluation

The cardiology consultant evaluated the patient with the finalized echocardiogram available for review. The consultant documented the following assessment: type II myocardial infarction consistent with demand ischemia from the stress of mechanical fall, without angina or ischemic electrocardiographic changes; chronic left anterior fascicular block present on prior electrocardiogram decades earlier; right bundle branch block new on this admission; hyperdynamic left ventricular function with ejection fraction of 71%. Regarding preoperative cardiovascular evaluation, the consultant documented: "She is stable from cardiovascular perspective to have general anesthesia and orthopedic surgery in the morning as planned. Perioperative cardiovascular risk is low, cannot be lowered further." Recommendations included telemetry monitoring, initiation of statin therapy, and: "Cleared for surgery tomorrow."

The cardiology consultant's conclusion was identical in clinical substance to the admitting resident's documented determination at 3:07 AM: no active cardiac condition, no barrier to surgery, perioperative cardiovascular risk low and not further reducible. The patient was medically cleared for operative fixation. Surgery did not occur that evening.

Day 2, 8:34 PM — Attending Attestation of H&P

The supervising attending attested the admitting resident's history and physical examination — 28 minutes after the cardiology consultant's clearance — documenting: "Discussed with resident and agree with the resident's findings and plan as documented in the resident's note." She additionally documented that she had independently interviewed and examined the patient, reviewed vital signs and laboratory work, and personally viewed the electrocardiogram. The resident's documented plan had included the cardiology consultation as a pending item — not as a surgical gating requirement. This attestation constitutes a documented statement of agreement with a plan whose central clinical conclusion — no unstable cardiac condition requiring routine delay — the attending had rejected verbally twice: once before the H&P was written, when the resident had pushed back and been overruled, and once after reading the resident's documented conclusion, when she had repeated the requirement without engagement. The attestation was executed 28 minutes after the cardiology consultant confirmed what the resident had documented at 3:07 AM. Surgery had not occurred. The patient remained pre-operative.

Day 3, 0015 — Hypotensive Episode

A blood pressure of 79/44 mmHg was documented by the bedside nurse, obtained while the patient was sleeping. The patient was asymptomatic. Heart rate was 85 beats per minute. Oxygen saturation had been maintained above 94% throughout the hospitalization. Prior blood pressure readings across the admission had ranged from 108 to 155 mmHg systolic. No intravenous fluids were running. The patient remained pre-operative, NPO, and immobilized. The primary hemodynamic substrate, as subsequently documented by the admitting resident in the overnight note, was hypovolemia from substantial fracture-related blood loss — approximately 750 to 800 mL of occult hemorrhage into the fracture hematoma over the pre-operative period, compounded by sedating medications including quetiapine, trazodone, and fluoxetine. Hemoglobin would be found to have declined from 11.3 g/dL on admission to 8.8 g/dL on the morning of Day 3, a drop of 2.5 g/dL consistent with the estimated hemorrhagic volume.

Day 3, 12:42 AM — SecureChat Notification and Fluid Management

The bedside nurse initiated a SecureChat message to both the PGY-3 and PGY-1 residents, reporting the blood pressure of 79/44 mmHg, noting that the patient was sleeping and asymptomatic, and asking whether intravenous fluids should be running given the expired maintenance order. The PGY-1 responded in the SecureChat, recommending a manual blood pressure measurement first, then independently entered a 1-liter intravenous lactated Ringer's bolus order — before either resident had reached the bedside. The bedside nurse confirmed the bolus was already running and that the manual blood pressure had been obtained at 80/50 mmHg, confirming the automated reading. The PGY-1 made this clinical decision without knowledge of the admitting resident's graduated fluid protocol, the conservative fluid strategy documented and re-confirmed across two prior shifts, the attending's earlier expressed concern for possible heart failure, or the clinical framework governing this patient's fluid management from the time of admission.

Following this SecureChat exchange and the manual blood pressure confirmation, the bedside nurse, PGY-1, and PGY-3 met at the bedside. The 1-liter bolus was already running. The PGY-3 assessed the patient directly: heart rate had not exceeded 85 beats per minute at any point during the hospitalization; oxygen saturation had remained above 94% throughout; prior systolic blood pressure readings ranged from 108 to 155 mmHg; the patient was asymptomatic and sleeping. The clinical picture was consistent with hypovolemia from fracture hematoma blood loss compounded by the absence of intravenous fluids and sedating medications, in the context of possible diastolic physiology proceeding to emergent surgery.

Approximately 15 minutes after the initial SecureChat notification, having completed the bedside evaluation, the PGY-3 communicated a revised clinical recommendation in the SecureChat: "I'm reconsidering my previous recommendation. Please deliver 500 mL LR as bolus, and re-evaluate. If SBP remains <110 mmHg, let us know (likely to continue another 250mL bolus). Once stabilized (SBP >110 mmHg), resume LR at 75 mL/h." This graduated protocol — developed through direct patient assessment and structured clinical reflection — was internally consistent with the conservative fluid strategy governing this patient's management from admission, with the perioperative fluid stewardship evidence for elderly surgical patients with possible diastolic physiology, and with the attending's own prior expressed concern for possible heart failure, which was the primary clinical rationale supporting a reassessment-based rather than unrestricted volume loading approach.

Day 3, ~1:00 AM — Attending Intervention (Bypass Event 2)

Following the PGY-3's revised graduated protocol in the SecureChat, the supervising attending appeared in person at the PGY-3's workstation — at a nearby working station area rather than on the patient unit — and announced that she had ordered the 1-liter bolus, directly contradicting the PGY-3's graduated protocol. She walked away. No clinical dialogue occurred. No engagement with the graduated protocol took place. No questions were asked. No explanation of clinical reasoning was offered.

This was the second time in this hospitalization that the supervising attending appeared in person and delivered a unilateral clinical determination to the admitting resident without engaging his documented clinical reasoning. At approximately 6:00 AM on Day 2, she had appeared at the end of the resident's overnight shift, read his documented contrary conclusion, and repeated the cardiology clearance requirement without dialogue. At approximately 1:00 AM on Day 3, she appeared at his workstation, announced the fluid decision, and departed without dialogue. The mechanism was identical on both occasions: in-person, synchronous, face-to-face communication used to deliver a unilateral determination rather than to initiate clinical exchange.

The PGY-3 subsequently documented the attending's intervention in the SecureChat: "just spoke with [attending] — please disregard." This message, written by the admitting resident in a system confirmed to constitute part of the formal medical record at this institution, represents a written, timestamped, legally discoverable record that the supervising attending intervened in this patient's fluid management following the PGY-3's revised protocol. The attending's clinical decision is captured in the permanent medical record — attributed to her by name, documented by the resident she had just informed. No independent documentation of the attending's clinical decision exists in the electronic health record under her own name. The 1-liter bolus order appears in the medication administration record under the PGY-1's name, the resident who had independently entered it before the attending's intervention. The attending's confirmation of and instruction regarding that order has no independent electronic health record documentation attributable to her.

The PGY-3 continued to monitor the patient's hemodynamic response: "once bolus is done can we get a repeat manual BP please?" At approximately 2:00 AM, the bedside nurse reported a blood pressure of 110/60 mmHg. The PGY-3 ordered maintenance fluids at 75 mL per hour — the endpoint specified in his graduated protocol — and requested that the overnight nursing team review his subsequent progress note to ensure solid handoff to the day shift.

Day 3, 5:40 AM — Overnight Progress Note (PGY-3)

The admitting resident completed the overnight progress note, documenting the nocturnal hypotensive episode, the hemodynamic response to fluid resuscitation, the hemoglobin decline attributable to fracture hematoma blood loss, a new acute kidney injury, and medication discontinuations. The note concluded: "Patient is medically optimized for surgery. Cleared to proceed to OR at 0815 pending acceptable coagulation studies and stable/improved hemoglobin on repeat labs."

Day 3, 6:56 AM — Attending Attestation of Overnight Note

The supervising attending cosigned the overnight note, agreeing with the admitting resident's clinical assessment and plan — her second documented attestation of agreement with the resident's clinical reasoning in this hospitalization.

Day 3, Morning — Preoperative Evaluations

The anesthesiologist assigned an ASA classification of 3, documented the cardiology clearance, and obtained informed consent for general anesthesia. The orthopedic surgeon confirmed the operative plan, obtained informed consent, and documented the surgical approach.

Day 3, 10:28 AM — Surgery

The patient underwent closed reduction of the right distal radius fracture and closed reduction internal fixation of the right intertrochanteric hip fracture using a cephalomedullary nail construct. Anesthesia was general. Estimated blood loss was 3 mL. There were no complications. The patient tolerated the procedure and was transferred to the recovery room in stable condition.

SECTION 3 — ANALYSIS

3a — Guideline Concordance Analysis

The attending physician's clinical decisions in this case are evaluated against the governing clinical guideline framework that existed, was publicly available, and was directly applicable to this patient's clinical situation at the time the decisions were made. The 2014 ACC/AHA Guideline on Perioperative Cardiovascular Evaluation and Management of Patients Undergoing Noncardiac Surgery is the authoritative standard for exactly this clinical scenario — an elderly patient with cardiac risk considerations requiring urgent operative intervention. Its application to the decisions made in this case is not inferential. It is direct. The admitting resident applied it explicitly at 3:07 AM and documented his application in the permanent medical record. The attending's decisions are evaluated against the same standard.

The guideline framework: emergent and urgent surgery

The 2014 ACC/AHA guideline establishes a stepwise algorithm for perioperative cardiovascular evaluation. Its first and most fundamental branch point addresses surgical urgency. For patients requiring emergent or urgent surgery, the guideline is explicit: the clinical team should proceed to the operating room without delay for pre-operative cardiac evaluation, managing identified cardiac risk factors in the perioperative and postoperative periods. Pre-operative cardiac evaluation is reserved for patients in whom the urgency of surgery permits the time required for evaluation and intervention, and in whom identified findings would change perioperative management in a manner that improves outcomes.

This patient required emergent operative fixation of a traumatic intertrochanteric hip fracture. The emergent nature of the procedure was documented by the admitting resident at 3:07 AM and was not contested by any subsequent provider. The admitting resident explicitly addressed the guideline framework in the H&P under a dedicated section titled "Concise pre-operative cardiac risk assessment," documenting: "Time-sensitive hip fracture repair should not be delayed for routine extensive cardiac testing." Under the ACC/AHA guideline's first branch point, the clinical team was directed to proceed to the operating room. The algorithm's subsequent steps — risk stratification, functional capacity assessment, non-invasive testing — are not reached for emergent surgical patients. They are downstream of a branch point this patient never met the criteria to cross.

The resident's risk stratification: objective and quantified

The admitting resident did not rely solely on clinical gestalt in reaching his guideline-concordant conclusion. He calculated and documented an RCRI score of 0, corresponding to a 30-day major cardiac event risk of 0.5–3.9% — a validated, objective, widely-used perioperative cardiac risk stratification tool. A score of 0 reflects the lowest risk category and provides no basis for pre-operative cardiac evaluation beyond clinical assessment. He additionally documented a Preoperative Mortality Predictor score of 9, reflecting low perioperative mortality risk. These quantified risk assessments, embedded in the 3:07 AM H&P alongside the guideline-concordant narrative conclusion, represent an objective evidentiary foundation for the resident's clinical determination that no pre-operative cardiac evaluation was warranted.

The cardiology consultation requirement: not indicated

The attending's requirement for cardiology consultation prior to surgery was not consistent with the ACC/AHA guideline framework for this patient. The guideline reserves pre-operative cardiac evaluation for patients with identified active cardiac conditions — a defined category that includes decompensated heart failure, unstable coronary syndromes, significant arrhythmias, and severe valvular disease. This patient met none of these criteria. Each potential active cardiac condition is considered in turn:

Decompensated heart failure: The admitting resident documented the absence of clinical signs of decompensated heart failure at 3:07 AM. The echocardiogram ordered by the resident and finalized at 12:58 PM demonstrated a hyperdynamic ejection fraction of 71%, categorically excluding heart failure with reduced ejection fraction. Diastolic function was formally read as normal by the interpreting cardiologist. Left atrial volume index was 28 mL/m², within the normal reference range, arguing against chronically elevated filling pressures. The average E/e' ratio was 12, below the threshold of 14 associated with elevated filling pressures. The attending's stated concern — undiagnosed heart failure — was a suspicion unsupported by any clinical, radiographic, or echocardiographic finding present at any point in the hospitalization.

Elevated pulmonary artery systolic pressure: The echocardiogram documented a moderately increased pulmonary artery systolic pressure of 50 mmHg. This finding warrants explicit acknowledgment. Pulmonary hypertension — even when moderate — does not constitute an active cardiac condition under the ACC/AHA perioperative guideline framework. The defined active cardiac conditions are decompensated heart failure, unstable coronary syndromes, significant arrhythmias, and severe valvular disease. Pulmonary hypertension is not among them. Critically, the cardiology consultant — who reviewed the complete echocardiographic dataset including the pulmonary artery pressure of 50 mmHg — concluded that no active cardiac condition was present and that perioperative cardiovascular risk was low and could not be lowered further.

Unstable coronary syndromes: The troponin elevation documented on admission showed a pattern of 0.06, 0.05, and 0.06 ng/mL across serial measurements. The admitting resident characterized this as most consistent with demand myocardial injury from the acute fracture and hospitalization. The cardiology consultant independently characterized this as type II myocardial infarction consistent with demand ischemia from the stress of the mechanical fall. A type II MI in the absence of angina, ischemic electrocardiographic changes, or regional wall motion abnormalities does not constitute an unstable coronary syndrome under any recognized diagnostic framework.

Significant arrhythmias: The patient was in normal sinus rhythm throughout her hospitalization. Heart rate did not exceed 85 beats per minute at any point. The electrocardiogram demonstrated right bundle branch block — new compared to a prior tracing from decades earlier — and left anterior fascicular block, which had been present on the prior tracing. No significant arrhythmia was identified by any provider or monitoring system.

Severe valvular disease: The echocardiogram demonstrated mild mitral regurgitation and mild tricuspid regurgitation, both characterized as consistent with age-related annular changes. The aortic valve was structurally normal with a valve area of 2.02 cm². No valvular abnormality was identified as clinically significant by any provider.

The cardiology consultation was therefore not indicated under the governing guideline at any point in this patient's hospitalization. The attending's stated rationale — BNP 202.8 pg/mL and concern for undiagnosed heart failure — does not constitute an active cardiac condition under the ACC/AHA framework.

The fluid management decision: internally contradictory

The attending's stated clinical concern — possible undiagnosed heart failure — was the precise reasoning that most strongly supported the admitting resident's conservative fluid approach and most strongly argued against the attending's own fluid management decision. The cardinal perioperative risk of fluid administration in a patient with possible diastolic physiology is acute decompensation from volume overload, manifesting as flash pulmonary edema. Enhanced Recovery After Surgery perioperative guidelines and anesthesiology consensus statements consistently recommend restrictive or goal-directed fluid strategies in patients with known or suspected cardiac dysfunction.

The admitting resident's graduated fluid protocol — 500 mL bolus with structured reassessment, conditional escalation, resumption of maintenance once hemodynamic stability was achieved — was precisely aligned with this evidence base and internally consistent with the attending's own stated concern. The attending's confirmation of the PGY-1's 1-liter bolus without a structured reassessment protocol was not consistent with this evidence base, not consistent with the conservative fluid strategy confirmed by two providers across two prior shifts, and not internally consistent with the attending's own expressed concern. The attending's two clinical interventions — requiring cardiology consultation for suspected heart failure and confirming a 1-liter bolus without reassessment in a patient with suspected heart failure — were mutually contradictory and each independently discordant with the evidence base governing their respective clinical domains.

The cardiology consultant's assessment as external validation

The cardiology consultant — a board-certified cardiologist — evaluated this patient with the finalized echocardiogram available, including all findings noted above. He applied the same clinical and guideline framework to the same clinical data and reached the conclusion that no active cardiac condition was present, no barrier to surgery existed, and perioperative cardiovascular risk was low and could not be lowered further. He cleared the patient for surgery the following morning. This conclusion was identical in clinical substance to the admitting resident's 3:07 AM documentation.

The cardiology consultant did not identify any finding the admitting resident had overlooked, any risk the resident's assessment had failed to address, or any clinical consideration that justified the delay the consultation had required. The external validator invoked by the attending to supersede the resident's assessment instead confirmed it. The attending's bypass of the resident's assessment was not a correction of a clinical deficit. It was a substitution of an unverified attending determination for a correct resident assessment — one that required a specialist consultation, an operative delay, and a nocturnal hemodynamic episode to confirm what the resident had documented at 3:07 AM.

The attending's attestation as documentary confirmation

Twenty-eight minutes after the cardiology consultant cleared the patient for surgery, the supervising attending attested the admitting resident's history and physical examination, documenting her agreement with the resident's findings and plan. The resident's plan had included cardiology consultation as a pending item — not as a surgical gating requirement. Whether this attestation constitutes a deliberate reversal or a routine cosignature executed without full awareness of its implications is a question this manuscript does not need to resolve. The documentary record speaks for itself: the attending stated a clinical requirement, read a contrary documented conclusion, repeated the requirement, required a specialist to address her concern, and 28 minutes after that specialist confirmed the resident's conclusion, signed a statement agreeing with it.

3b — Causal Chain and Patient-Level Harm Analysis

The clinical sequence documented in this case is not a collection of independent events. It constitutes a traceable causal chain in which a bypass-generated decision initiated an adverse outcome pathway whose downstream consequences were compounded by a second bypass event that managed a problem the first had plausibly created. Each link in that chain is supported by the clinical record and by the peer-reviewed literature governing the management of elderly patients with traumatic hip fractures. Where causal relationships are plausible but not definitively established, they are characterized as such.

Link 1: Bypass event 1 generates a guideline-discordant operative delay

Before the admitting resident had completed the history and physical examination, the supervising attending communicated her requirement that cardiology clearance be obtained prior to surgery. The resident pushed back directly, arguing that preoperative cardiology clearance was not warranted. He was overruled. He subsequently documented his guideline-concordant contrary conclusion in the permanent medical record at 3:07 AM — including a dedicated section on pre-operative cardiac risk assessment, a calculated RCRI score of 0, and a verbatim statement that no evidence of STEMI, decompensated heart failure, or other unstable cardiac condition requiring routine delay was present. At approximately 6:00 AM, the attending reviewed the H&P, read this conclusion, and repeated the requirement without engaging a single element of its reasoning.

As established in Section 3a, the cardiology consultation requirement was not consistent with the 2014 ACC/AHA Guideline on Perioperative Cardiovascular Evaluation. For patients requiring emergent surgery, the guideline directs the clinical team to proceed to the operating room without pre-operative cardiac evaluation in the absence of active cardiac conditions. This patient met no defined criteria for an active cardiac condition at any point in her hospitalization. The consultation requirement introduced an operative delay that was not clinically justified by the governing guideline and was not supported by any clinical, echocardiographic, or laboratory finding present in the chart.

The operative delay was further compounded by a secondary failure: the cardiology consultation — required at approximately 6:00 AM and ordered by the admitting resident at 4:54 AM — had not been completed when the admitting resident returned to overnight coverage at 7:00 PM. The echocardiogram, finalized at 12:58 PM and available in the chart for more than six hours, had not prompted the daytime team to contact cardiology. When the admitting resident contacted the cardiology office upon returning to overnight coverage, he was informed that no prior consult call had been placed. The cardiology nurse practitioner independently confirmed the absence of prior contact. The cardiology note, created at 8:06 PM following the admitting resident's call, provides documentary corroboration that the consultation had not been initiated before his intervention.

Link 2: The delay persists beyond cardiology clearance

The echocardiogram ordered by the admitting resident at 3:07 AM was finalized at 12:58 PM on Day 2. As established in Section 3a, it categorically excluded both heart failure with reduced ejection fraction and the echocardiographic profile of heart failure with preserved ejection fraction. The attending's stated concern was resolved by the resident's own ordered workup before cardiology had been reached.

When the cardiology consultant evaluated the patient that evening, he found no active cardiac condition, no barrier to surgery, and no indication for intervention, documenting: "She is stable from cardiovascular perspective to have general anesthesia and orthopedic surgery in the morning as planned. Perioperative cardiovascular risk is low, cannot be lowered further." The patient was medically cleared. Surgery did not occur that evening. The operative delay extended beyond cardiology clearance into a second window of continued pre-operative waiting with no new clinical justification. Twenty-eight minutes after cardiology cleared the patient, the supervising attending attested the admitting resident's H&P, documenting her agreement with the resident's findings and plan.

Link 3: The delay produces a documented adverse physiologic substrate

The consequences of prolonged operative delay in elderly patients with traumatic hip fractures are among the most consistently documented findings in the orthopedic and geriatric literature. Multiple prospective cohort studies, systematic reviews, and national audit data demonstrate that operative delay beyond 24 to 48 hours in this population is independently associated with increased 30-day mortality, pneumonia, venous thromboembolism, pressure injury, and delirium after adjustment for baseline comorbidities. The American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons clinical practice guideline on management of hip fractures in the elderly assigns its strongest recommendation to timely surgical intervention.

This patient — 85 years old, NPO, immobilized with a traumatic hip and wrist fracture, recently bereaved, with documented depression, insomnia, and CKD stage 3a — represented the highest-risk end of the distribution for delay-associated morbidity. The admitting resident had explicitly identified delirium and fall risk mitigation as a primary clinical priority at 3:07 AM and had operationalized non-pharmacologic delirium precautions in his admission orders. Each additional hour of pre-operative delay directly undermined those precautions.

Both intravenous fluid orders placed during the pre-operative period reflected surgical anticipation orders calibrated to cover a pre-operative window each provider reasonably expected to be brief. The cardiology clearance requirement, by extending the operative delay beyond any provider's anticipated surgical window, systematically exhausted each fluid order placed in anticipation of surgery that did not occur on the expected timeline. The daytime hospitalist's maintenance fluid order expired at approximately 4:45 PM on Day 2. The patient subsequently had no intravenous fluids running during a pre-operative period that extended into the overnight hours of Day 3.

Link 4: The hemodynamic episode is plausibly downstream of multiple contributing delays

At 0015 on Day 3, a blood pressure of 79/44 mmHg was documented in a sleeping, asymptomatic patient. The clinical picture — absence of compensatory tachycardia, preserved oxygenation, prior systolic blood pressure range of 108 to 155 mmHg, asymptomatic presentation during sleep — was consistent with hypovolemia rather than hemodynamic compromise from a primary cardiac or septic etiology.

The hemodynamic substrate is documentable from the admitting resident's own overnight note. Hemoglobin declined from 11.3 g/dL on admission to 8.8 g/dL on the morning of Day 3 — a decline of 2.5 g/dL representing approximately 750 to 800 mL of occult hemorrhage into the fracture hematoma over the pre-operative period. The resident's assessment explicitly attributed the nocturnal hypotension to hypovolemia from this substantial fracture-related blood loss, compounded by sedating medications including quetiapine, trazodone, and fluoxetine.

The nocturnal hemodynamic episode was plausibly, though not definitively, downstream of multiple contributing delays. The primary contributor was the attending's requirement for cardiology clearance prior to surgery — a guideline-discordant decision made by circumventing the admitting resident's explicit, timestamped, contrary conclusion. A secondary contributor was the daytime team's failure to contact cardiology during daytime hours despite the consultation having been required and the echocardiogram having been available since 12:58 PM.

Link 5: Bypass event 2 manages a consequence of bypass event 1 through a fait accompli mechanism

The admitting PGY-3's graduated fluid protocol — developed through direct bedside assessment and 15 minutes of structured clinical reflection — was internally consistent with the conservative fluid strategy governing this patient's management from admission and with the perioperative fluid stewardship evidence for elderly surgical patients with possible diastolic physiology.

The attending's intervention did not take the form of a telephonic order or a written counter-instruction. She appeared in person at the admitting resident's workstation, announced that she had ordered the 1-liter bolus — directly contradicting the resident's graduated protocol — and walked away. No clinical dialogue occurred. The mechanism of this intervention is precisely characterized as a fait accompli: the clinical decision was presented as already made and already in progress at the moment the resident was informed of it, closing every channel for dialogue at the moment of disclosure.

The admitting resident documented the attending's intervention in the SecureChat: "just spoke with [attending] — please disregard." This message constitutes documentary evidence in the permanent medical record that the supervising attending intervened in this patient's fluid management. No independent documentation of this clinical decision exists in the electronic health record under the attending's own authorship.

The admitting resident subsequently monitored the patient's hemodynamic response, requested a repeat manual blood pressure confirmation, received a blood pressure of 110/60 mmHg, and implemented maintenance fluids at 75 mL/hr — the endpoint specified in his own graduated protocol. The attending's intervention implemented the initial bolus; the resident managed its outcome.

The complete causal chain

The five links form a closed and self-referential sequence: a bypass-generated, guideline-discordant cardiology consultation requirement initiated an operative delay before the history and physical examination was complete; that delay was extended by the daytime team's failure to execute the already-required consultation during daytime hours; the prolonged delay exhausted both fluid orders placed in anticipation of imminent surgery; progressive fracture hematoma hemorrhage accumulated over the pre-operative period without operative intervention; a nocturnal hemodynamic episode plausibly downstream of this cumulative delay required clinical management; and the clinical management of that episode was itself subject to a second bypass event whose mechanism — a fait accompli in-person announcement at the admitting resident's workstation — is documentable from the permanent medical record.

At no point in this sequence did any institutional mechanism exist to flag, review, intercept, or document the attending's clinical decisions as they were made.

3c — Systems Failure Analysis

The clinical events described in this case are not adequately characterized as a series of interpersonal communication failures between individual providers. Examined through the lens of patient safety systems science, they constitute a constellation of identifiable, nameable, and potentially remediable systems failures — latent conditions embedded in the structure of clinical supervision in academic medicine that enabled a bypass-generated adverse outcome pathway to traverse a full clinical episode without triggering a single existing detection or review mechanism.

Failure Mode 1: Removal of a documented safety barrier without review

Patient safety systems theory, articulated by Reason and subsequently operationalized in healthcare by Leape, Pronovost, and the Institute of Medicine's landmark To Err is Human framework, conceptualizes safety as a property of layered defensive barriers rather than individual provider performance. In this framework, the admitting resident's 3:07 AM history and physical examination functioned as a documented, reviewable, auditable safety barrier — a clinical reasoning artifact that explicitly identified the risk landscape, applied the governing guideline framework, reached a defensible and quantified conclusion, and operationalized that conclusion in a comprehensive order set.

The attending's bypass of this safety barrier occurred across two distinct interactions. Prior to 3:07 AM, before formally reviewing the resident's note, the attending communicated her concern to the admitting resident in person and issued a direct instruction: consult cardiology and require clearance prior to surgery. The resident contested this requirement directly, presenting a clinical counter-argument. He was overruled. He then documented his contrary clinical conclusion in the permanent medical record at 3:07 AM, preserving his guideline-concordant reasoning as a contemporaneous record of a clinical disagreement that had already occurred.

At approximately 6:00 AM, the attending reviewed the H&P — including the resident's explicit, timestamped conclusion. Having read that conclusion, she repeated the instruction. No question was posed to the resident at either interaction. No element of the resident's documented reasoning was addressed, challenged, or incorporated at either interaction. The attending was physically present with the H&P's author on both occasions. She had read the document. She restated her position without engaging its contents.

This sequence forecloses a critical interpretive alternative. The attending's instruction cannot be attributed to unfamiliarity with the resident's assessment — she had read it. It cannot be attributed to time pressure precluding dialogue — she interacted with the resident twice, in person. The safety barrier was not bypassed inadvertently. It was bypassed deliberately, by a provider who had read it, in the physical presence of its author, without engaging a single element of its reasoning, on two separate occasions.

No existing supervisory framework in graduate medical education requires attending physicians to document their clinical rationale when overriding a resident's assessment. No quality metric tracks the guideline concordance of attending decisions made at shift transitions. No reporting mechanism flags the removal of a documented resident safety barrier as an event requiring review. The barrier was removed twice. The system did not notice either time.

Failure Mode 2: Shift handoff as a latent vulnerability

The timing of bypass event 1's second interaction was not incidental. The attending's repetition of the cardiology clearance requirement occurred at the end of the admitting resident's overnight shift — the precise moment at which the resident was least available for sustained dialogue and most structurally disadvantaged in any implicit hierarchy negotiation with an attending physician.

Bypass at shift handoff is structurally complete in a way that bypass during a shared shift is not. The attending's decision was implemented in a clinical environment from which its primary challenger had departed. Its downstream effects — the operative delay extending through the day shift, the daytime hospitalist's documentation of "cardiology clearance pending" as a surgical gate, the expiration of fluid orders calibrated to a surgical timeline that did not materialize — all unfolded across hours in which the admitting resident had no visibility and no recourse. Existing handoff safety frameworks — the I-PASS protocol, Joint Commission National Patient Safety Goals on handoff communication — address information transfer between outgoing and incoming providers. None address the vulnerability created when an attending uses the handoff transition to issue clinical counter-determinations to a departing resident without engagement.

Failure Mode 3: Fait accompli bypass as a patient safety mechanism

At approximately 1:00 AM on Day 3, following the PGY-3's revised graduated fluid protocol in the SecureChat, the supervising attending appeared in person at the admitting resident's workstation and announced that she had ordered the 1-liter bolus, directly contradicting the resident's graduated protocol. She walked away. No clinical dialogue occurred. No engagement with the graduated protocol took place. No questions were asked.

This bypass mechanism is precisely characterized as a fait accompli: the clinical decision was presented as already made and already in progress at the moment the resident was informed of it. The announcement was not an invitation to clinical exchange. It was a notification of an irreversible decision — delivered in person, with immediate departure, closing every channel for dialogue at the moment of disclosure.

The fait accompli mechanism has a specific clinical significance in this case. The admitting resident had just completed a bedside evaluation, developed a structured clinical protocol through 15 minutes of reflection, and communicated that protocol through the documented clinical communication channel available to him. The attending appeared, announced the contrary decision, and departed — without having conducted a concurrent bedside evaluation, without reviewing the protocol the resident had just developed, and without engaging any of the clinical reasoning that had produced it.

The SecureChat record captures the fait accompli in the permanent medical record with documentary precision. The admitting resident's message — "just spoke with [attending] — please disregard" — is a written, timestamped, legally discoverable record in a system confirmed to constitute part of the formal medical record at this institution. The attending's clinical decision is captured in the permanent record by the resident she informed. No independent documentation of this decision exists in the electronic health record under the attending's own authorship.

Failure Mode 4: Undocumented attending clinical decision

The attending's clinical decision to confirm the 1-liter bolus exists nowhere in the medical record under her own authorship. The 1-liter bolus order appears in the medication administration record under the PGY-1's name. The attending's role in confirming and endorsing that order is unattributable from the documented record to its actual decision-maker.

This creates a documentation accountability failure with direct patient safety implications. An attending clinical decision that does not appear in the medical record under the attending's authorship cannot be reviewed for guideline concordance, cannot be incorporated into a root cause analysis, and cannot be attributed to its actual author in any subsequent quality or safety process. Joint Commission standards require that verbal orders be authenticated in the medical record within a defined timeframe. Whether the attending's in-person instruction constitutes a verbal order requiring authentication under applicable standards is a question the institution's patient safety and compliance infrastructure would need to determine.

Failure Mode 5: Absence of institutional detection and reporting mechanisms

The causal chain documented in Section 3b traversed the full clinical episode without activating a single existing institutional detection or review mechanism. No trigger flagged the cardiology consultation as guideline-discordant for an emergent surgical patient without active cardiac conditions. No alert identified the operative delay as extending beyond evidence-based thresholds for elderly hip fracture patients. No system detected the expiration of both pre-operative fluid orders. No mechanism recorded the attending's in-person announcement as a clinical event requiring documentation or review. No reporting pathway enabled the admitting resident to document either bypass event as a patient safety occurrence requiring institutional attention.

The ACGME's Clinical Learning Environment Review program — CLER — explicitly evaluates how teaching hospitals integrate patient safety culture with graduate medical education, identifying supervision culture as a patient safety variable requiring institutional assessment. The events documented here represent precisely the category of systemic vulnerability CLER's site visit methodology is designed to surface. That they occurred without institutional detection, without triggering existing reporting systems, and without producing any formal review or corrective mechanism is itself a finding that CLER's focus area on patient safety culture would characterize as a gap requiring remediation.

The absence of detection is not a passive feature of this case. It is an active systems failure — the predictable consequence of a supervisory architecture designed to protect residents from insufficient attending oversight, but never designed to detect, review, or remediate the inverse risk: attending clinical decisions made without resident engagement that are less safe, less guideline-concordant, and less internally consistent than the decisions they displace.

The pattern across both bypass events

Perhaps the most significant systems observation available in this case is the documentable pattern across both bypass events. At approximately 6:00 AM on Day 2 and at approximately 1:00 AM on Day 3 — separated by approximately 19 hours — the supervising attending employed the same mechanism: appearing in person, delivering a unilateral clinical determination without engaging the resident's reasoning, and departing. At 6:00 AM she had read a comprehensive, guideline-concordant, quantified clinical assessment and repeated a contrary requirement without addressing a single element of its reasoning. At 1:00 AM she appeared at the admitting resident's workstation following a bedside evaluation and a structured clinical protocol, announced a contrary decision already in progress, and walked away.

The 3:07 AM history and physical examination documents the first bypass event in the permanent medical record. The SecureChat documents the second. Both bypass events are documentable from the permanent medical record. Both followed the same mechanism. Neither generated an institutional response. The pattern is not incidental. It is a supervisory behavior that repeated across a single hospitalization without detection, without review, and without any mechanism by which the institution could have known it had occurred.

SECTION 4 — DISCUSSION

This case contributes to the patient safety literature in a specific and bounded way. It does not establish the prevalence of resident bypass, characterize its frequency across institutions, or quantify the proportion of bypass events that produce adverse outcomes. It does something more foundational: it documents, with timestamped primary source evidence across a complete clinical episode, that bypass can function as a patient safety failure mode — one with a reproducible mechanism, a traceable causal chain, and an institutional detection gap that existing supervisory and reporting frameworks are structurally unable to address.

Why bypass has been framed as an educational problem

The existing literature on resident bypass has developed almost exclusively within the graduate medical education community, where the primary analytical lens is competency attainment and the primary outcome of interest is trainee development. Within this framework, bypass is harmful because it denies residents the deliberate practice required for milestone progression, disrupts the trust calibration process through which attending physicians make entrustment decisions, and transmits a hidden curriculum message that undermines professional identity formation. These harms are real, cumulative, and consequential for the quality of the physician workforce over time.

The educational framing has, however, produced a category error in how bypass is understood institutionally. By characterizing bypass as a training environment problem — one affecting the resident — it has situated the primary accountability for addressing bypass within graduate medical education oversight structures: program directors, clinical competency committees, ACGME requirements. These structures are designed to protect residents from inadequate supervision. They are not designed to evaluate the clinical quality of attending decisions made during bypass events, to assess whether those decisions represent improvements on the resident reasoning they displace, or to detect when bypass-generated clinical decisions produce patient-level harm. The result is an accountability gap that is not incidental to the existing framework — it is structurally inherent to it.

What a patient safety framing adds

Reframing bypass as a patient safety phenomenon — rather than, or in addition to, an educational one — has specific and practical implications for institutional accountability. Patient safety reporting frameworks, adverse event investigation protocols, root cause analysis methodology, and quality improvement infrastructure are all designed to evaluate clinical decisions and their outcomes, not the training environment in which they occur. They apply to attending physicians and residents alike. They are triggered by patient-level events rather than trainee-level metrics. And they produce institutional responses — corrective action, process redesign, accountability documentation — that graduate medical education frameworks are not equipped to generate.

In this case, the patient safety framing reveals consequences that the educational framing cannot see. The guideline-discordant cardiology consultation requirement is not primarily an educational harm — it is a clinical decision that can be evaluated against the 2014 ACC/AHA Perioperative Cardiovascular Evaluation Guideline and found to be discordant. The operative delay is not primarily a training environment deficit — it is a patient outcome that can be assessed against the hip fracture delay literature and found to represent excess risk exposure. The attending's undocumented clinical decision — confirmed in the permanent record only through the admitting resident's SecureChat relay message — is not primarily a supervisory communication failure; it is a documentation accountability failure with implications for medical record integrity and institutional quality review.

None of these consequences were detectable by the institution's existing mechanisms. None triggered a reporting event. None produced a corrective response. The patient safety framing does not simply add a new lens to an existing problem. It reveals a category of consequence that the existing lens cannot resolve.

The inverse supervision risk

The concept of an inverse supervision risk — attending clinical decisions that are less safe than the resident decisions they displace — has not been characterized in the existing literature on clinical supervision or patient safety. The supervision literature has addressed the risk of insufficient attending oversight: residents acting with inadequate guidance, making clinical errors that an engaged attending would have prevented. The ACGME CLER program, the Joint Commission's standards on supervision, and the patient safety literature on trainee errors all address this risk from the same direction.

This case addresses the opposite direction. The admitting resident's clinical assessments — the 3:07 AM history and physical examination and the graduated fluid protocol — were, on the available evidence, more guideline-concordant, more internally consistent, and more evidence-aligned than the attending decisions that displaced them. The attending's cardiology clearance requirement was not indicated by the governing guideline. Her fluid management decision was internally contradictory to her own stated clinical concern. The specialist she required confirmed the resident's assessment. The resident's graduated protocol endpoint was ultimately achieved through his own management of the attending's intervention's aftermath.

The inverse supervision risk is not a claim that attending physicians are systematically less clinically capable than the residents they supervise. It is a claim that the act of bypass — the removal of a resident's clinical reasoning from the workflow without engagement — introduces a specific risk that is independent of the relative clinical capabilities of the providers involved. When an attending bypasses a resident, she substitutes an unverified clinical determination for a documented one. Without dialogue, without documentation, and without any institutional mechanism to evaluate the substitution, there is no way to know whether the substitution represents an improvement. In this case, the external validator the attending invoked established that it did not.

Generalizability and structural conditions

The specific facts of this case are not generalizable. The structural conditions that enabled it are. The shift handoff vulnerability, the absence of any requirement for attending physicians to document their rationale when overriding a resident's documented assessment, the gap in patient safety reporting frameworks that leaves bypass-generated adverse outcomes invisible to institutional review, and the absence of any feedback mechanism by which a resident learns that a specialist has confirmed his assessment — none of these are idiosyncratic to this institution. They are features of the supervisory architecture of academic medicine broadly, and they are reproducible conditions for the events this case documents.

The SecureChat documentation in this case is worth noting as a specific generalizability observation. The attending's intervention was captured in the permanent medical record only because the admitting resident relayed her announcement in a documented communication channel. In the absence of that relay message, the attending's clinical decision would exist nowhere in the medical record under any attribution. Most bypass events do not produce a contemporaneous written record of the attending's intervention. Most bypass events that generate adverse outcomes are therefore invisible not only to institutional review but to the medical record itself.

Limitations

Several limitations constrain the conclusions available from this case. It is a single case from a single institution, and no causal inference about the frequency, distribution, or institutional prevalence of bypass-generated adverse outcomes can be drawn from a single observation. The causal relationship between the bypass-generated operative delay and the nocturnal hemodynamic episode is plausible and physiologically coherent but not definitively established; alternative explanations — including the independent contribution of fracture hematoma blood loss and sedating medications, which the admitting resident himself identified as primary contributors — cannot be excluded as the sole sufficient cause. The attending's clinical reasoning was not formally documented beyond her stated concern for undiagnosed heart failure; it is possible that additional clinical considerations, not captured in the available record, informed her decisions in ways that cannot be assessed from the documentation alone. The daytime team's failure to contact cardiology during daytime hours is supported by three independent lines of evidence but has not been confirmed from a formal chart notation and is characterized accordingly. Finally, the SecureChat system's status as part of the formal medical record may vary by institution, and the documentary significance attributed to the relay message in this analysis may not generalize to settings where such communications are not retained as part of the legal medical record.

These limitations do not undermine the case's central contribution. They define its boundaries. A single case with primary source documentation across a complete clinical episode establishes proof of concept — that bypass can function as a patient safety failure mode with a traceable causal chain — and generates hypotheses that prospective study designs can test. The contribution is foundational rather than definitive, and it is offered as such.

SECTION 5 — RECOMMENDATIONS

The systems failures documented in this case are not attributable to individual provider malice, incompetence, or indifference. They are attributable to a supervisory architecture that was not designed to detect the inverse supervision risk, not equipped to evaluate the clinical quality of attending decisions made during bypass events, and not structured to produce institutional accountability when those decisions generate patient-level harm. The recommendations that follow are directed at systems and institutions rather than individuals.

Recommendation 1: Incorporate resident bypass into existing patient safety event reporting frameworks

Resident bypass events — specifically those in which an attending physician overrides a documented resident clinical assessment without engagement and through a mechanism that excludes the resident from the decision — should be reportable through existing patient safety event reporting systems, not solely through graduate medical education channels. Institutions should add resident bypass as a reportable event type in existing safety reporting systems, with a structured data field capturing the nature of the bypassed assessment, the attending's substituted decision, and any patient-level outcomes associated with the decision. Residents should have explicit, protected access to this reporting pathway, with assurance that bypass reports will be reviewed by patient safety leadership rather than solely by graduate medical education administration.

Recommendation 2: Require attending documentation when overriding a resident's formally documented clinical assessment

When an attending physician issues a clinical counter-determination that contradicts a resident's formally documented assessment — particularly in the context of a time-sensitive surgical patient — institutional policy should require the attending to document the clinical rationale for the override in the medical record under her own authorship. This documentation requirement creates an auditable record of the attending's clinical reasoning, enables retrospective guideline concordance review, ensures that the clinical decision is attributed to its actual author, and creates a moment of deliberate reflection that may itself reduce reflexive bypass.

Recommendation 3: Establish guideline concordance review for attending orders that override documented resident assessments in time-sensitive surgical patients

Institutions should establish a mechanism for retrospective guideline concordance review of attending clinical decisions that override documented resident assessments in time-sensitive surgical patients — particularly decisions involving pre-operative consultation requirements, operative delay, and perioperative fluid management. The 2014 ACC/AHA Perioperative Cardiovascular Evaluation Guideline, the AAOS hip fracture management guideline, and comparable specialty-specific perioperative guidelines provide objective, accessible standards against which attending decisions can be evaluated retrospectively.

Recommendation 4: Align shift handoff policy with bypass prevention

Attending physicians who issue clinical counter-determinations at the time of resident shift handoff — particularly counter-determinations that contradict a formally documented resident assessment — should be required to notify the incoming coverage team of the override and its rationale, and to document the decision in the medical record before the departing resident's shift ends. This policy intervention does not require attending physicians to defer to resident judgment. It requires them to ensure that counter-determinations made at the moment of maximum resident vulnerability are documented, communicated, and attributable before the clinical environment they will govern is handed off.

Recommendation 5: Integrate bypass identification into ACGME Clinical Learning Environment Review self-study and faculty development curricula

The ACGME CLER program evaluates teaching hospitals across six focus areas including patient safety, quality improvement, supervision, and transitions of care. Institutions preparing CLER self-study documentation should explicitly address whether mechanisms exist for residents to report bypass events through patient safety channels; whether bypass-associated clinical decisions are subject to guideline concordance review; and whether faculty development programs specifically address the inverse supervision risk.

Recommendation 6: Close the feedback loop between consultant assessments and resident clinical reasoning

Institutions should establish a mechanism — implementable within existing electronic health record infrastructure — to notify the admitting resident when a consultant's assessment materially addresses the clinical question that prompted the bypass event. In this case, the cardiology consultant's assessment confirmed the admitting resident's clinical reasoning in every material respect. The resident was never formally notified of this confirmation. This notification serves both educational and patient safety functions: it closes the feedback loop essential for clinical reasoning development and creates an institutional record that the bypass-generated consultation produced no new clinical information.

SECTION 6 — CONCLUSION

At 3:07 AM, an internal medicine resident completed a history and physical examination for an 85-year-old woman who had broken her hip and lost her husband of 64 years two days before her fall. The document he produced was comprehensive, individualized, and guideline-concordant. It enumerated every cardiac risk consideration present in the chart, applied a validated perioperative risk stratification tool, dedicated a section to the ACC/AHA perioperative guideline framework, and reached a verbatim conclusion that no evidence of an unstable cardiac condition requiring routine delay of time-sensitive hip fracture surgery was present. It simultaneously ordered the echocardiogram, the repeat electrocardiogram, the cardiology consultation, the conservative fluid strategy, the multimodal analgesia protocol, the renal-protective medication plan, the electrolyte targets, the non-pharmacologic delirium precautions, and the social work evaluation for bereavement support. It was the clinical document of a physician in training doing exactly what graduate medical education is designed to produce.

That document was bypassed — twice, by the same attending, using the same mechanism, across the same hospitalization. The first bypass, at approximately 6:00 AM, converted a resident-ordered pending consultation into a pre-operative surgical gate and initiated an operative delay that the governing clinical guideline did not support. The second bypass, at approximately 1:00 AM the following morning, overrode a graduated fluid protocol developed through direct bedside assessment and structured clinical reflection, through a fait accompli announcement at the resident's workstation that foreclosed clinical dialogue at the moment of disclosure.

The specialist invoked to justify the first bypass confirmed the resident's assessment. The attending attested agreement with the resident's plan 28 minutes later. The second bypass is documented in the permanent medical record by the resident's own relay message in a formal communication system — the only record, under any authorship, that the attending's intervention occurred at all. Neither bypass event generated an institutional response. Neither triggered a reporting mechanism. Neither produced a corrective action. The patient underwent surgery the following morning without complications — but only after an operative delay, a nocturnal hemodynamic episode plausibly attributable to that delay, and a fluid management intervention that was less internally consistent with the attending's own stated clinical concern than the protocol it displaced.

The 3:07 AM document is still in the chart. It remains what it was when it was written: a complete, accurate, compassionate clinical assessment, produced in the early hours of the morning for a woman who was frightened, in pain, and grieving. It documented a clinical disagreement that had already occurred before it was written. It preserved a guideline-concordant conclusion that was overridden before it could govern. It was attested — as agreed with — by the attending who had overridden it, 28 minutes after the specialist she required had confirmed it.

Resident bypass is not a supervision style. It is not a communication preference. It is the removal of a clinical reasoning process from the workflow of a patient's care — a process that in this case was documented, quantified, guideline-grounded, and confirmed by independent specialist review. When that removal produces a guideline-discordant decision, an operative delay, and a hemodynamic episode in an 85-year-old woman with a broken hip and no one left at home, it is a patient safety event. It should be recognized as one, reported as one, reviewed as one, and remediated as one.

The institutional infrastructure to do this does not yet exist. Building it is the work this case calls for.
